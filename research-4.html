Investigating Protein Interactions in Prostate Cancer

Project Overview

In collaboration with a Ph.D. candidate at Johns Hopkins University, I researched how carbon dioxide (CO₂) emissions influence malaria transmission across African nations. By combining environmental and socioeconomic data, I used machine learning models such as random forest and gradient boosting regression to uncover complex, nonlinear patterns in disease spread. The results showed a positive correlation between CO₂ emissions and malaria prevalence, particularly in tropical regions where microclimate changes enhance mosquito survival. Meanwhile, socioeconomic factors like improved sanitation helped mitigate malaria risk across environments. This study offered new insights into how climate change and social conditions interact to shape malaria dynamics, contributing to future climate-resilient public health strategies.
