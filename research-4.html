Exploring the Relationship Between Carbon Dioxide Emissions and Malaria in Africa

Abstract

In this project, I investigated how carbon dioxide (CO₂) emissions influence malaria transmission across African nations. By combining environmental and socioeconomic data, I applied machine learning models, including random forest and gradient boosting regression, to uncover complex, nonlinear patterns in disease spread. My results revealed a positive correlation between CO₂ emissions and malaria prevalence, especially in tropical regions where microclimate changes enhance mosquito survival. Socioeconomic factors such as improved sanitation also mitigated malaria risk. This study provided insights into how climate change and social conditions interact to affect malaria dynamics, contributing to strategies for climate-resilient public health.

Contribution

I independently designed and conducted this research, performing data collection, integration, and analysis. I implemented machine learning models to analyze environmental and socioeconomic factors affecting malaria spread, interpreted results, and drew conclusions connecting climate and social conditions to disease dynamics.
